\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

% Motivation
In computer graphics, a common approach to model complex materials is to approximate them as an idealized reflector permutated by sub-geometric details.
%
When these sub-geometric details are macroscopic meaning bigger than a single pixel on the screen, they can be simulated using a normal map.
%
On the other hand, details that are significantly smaller than a pixel are called microfacets and are usually stochastically approximated through microfacet models like GGX.
%
However, between these two approaches lies the mesoscopic phenomenon of glints, which is important to accurately simulate the glittering effects of snow or flaky car paints.
%
This phenomenon can not simply be reproduced using statistical microfacet models because the microfacets are big enough that the viewer can identify individual facets reflecting.


% Problem
This renders two important requirements that a visually plausible glint renderer must meet:
%
\begin{itemize}
    \item Glints must be temporally and spatially coherent (this can be relaxed between different levels of detail)
    \item With high facet density the result must converge into a smooth microfacet model
\end{itemize}
